## Computational complexity theory

I find the theory of [*computational complexity*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_complexity_theory)
riveting&mdash;see, e.g., [Arora, Barak, 2009].
I especially like [*parameterized complexity theory*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameterized_complexity).

Other topics related to computational complexity that have caught my interest
in particular are
- the [*Polynomial Hierarchy*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_hierarchy),
- *subexponential-time complexity* and the [*ETH*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_time_hypothesis),
- *compilability*&mdash;see, e.g., [Cadoli, Donini, Liberatore, Schaerf, 2002], and
- [*non-uniform (or circuit) complexity*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circuit_complexity).

I studied the interaction of these topics
from a parameterized complexity perspective
in my [PhD thesis](http://ronalddehaan.eu/files/Dissertation_deHaan_2016.pdf)
[De Haan, 2016].

## Computational social choice

I am also fascinated by many topics in the area of
[*computational social choice*](http://research.illc.uva.nl/COMSOC/what-is-comsoc.html)&mdash;see,
e.g., [Brandt, Conitzer, Endriss, Lang, Procaccia, 2016]&mdash;especially
by their computational aspects.

Some topics in computational social choice that I like in particular are:
- *judgment aggregation*,
- *voting (in combinatorial domains)*,
- *fair allocation*, and
- *stable matching*.


## Compactness/efficiency tradeoffs in representation languages

I like to think about representation languages&mdash;in different
domains&mdash;and their tradeoffs between compactness (or succinctness)
and expressivity,
one the one hand, and computational efficiency, on the other hand.

Such tradeoffs often come up in the following areas:
- [*knowledge representation and reasoning*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_representation_and_reasoning),
- [*knowledge compilation*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_compilation)&mdash;see [Darwiche, Marquis, 2002],
- *logics*&mdash;see [below](#logics),
- *symbolic AI*&mdash;see [below](#symbolic-ai), and
- [*declarative programming*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declarative_programming).

These tradeoffs also appear in less likely places,
such as in the framework of *judgment aggregation*
in *computational social choice*&mdash;see [Endriss, Grandi, De Haan, Lang, 2016].

## Complexity arguments in cognitive science

Computational complexity arguments have been used in the field of
[*(computational) cognitive science*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_cognition)
to argue that (certain) cognitive theories that aim to explain various cognitive capacities
need refinement, explaining how humans' cognitive performance&mdash;claimed by
these theories&mdash;matches the limited amount of time that humans need for this.
For more details on such computational complexity arguments
in cognitive science, see [Van Rooij, Blokpoel, Kwisthout, Wareham, 2019].

I like to study various cognitive theories from a computational complexity perspective,
which helps to indicate which of these theories suffer from the explanatory gap
mentioned above.
For this, I use the classical theory of
[*complexity theory*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_complexity_theory),
as well as
[*parameterized complexity theory*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameterized_complexity).

Additionally, the notion of
[*instance complexity*](https://complexityzoo.uwaterloo.ca/Complexity_Zoo:I#iclogpoly)&mdash;which
can be based on concepts from
[*Kolmogorov complexity*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kolmogorov_complexity)&mdash;might
turn out to be useful for classifying computational aspects of cognitive theories.


## Automated reasoning

The area of [*automated reasoning*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automated_reasoning)
has seen enormous progress over the last few decades&mdash;so
much so that automated reasoning tools are now routinely used to solve industrial-sized
inputs for problems that are theoretically classified as computationally intractable.
This is especially true for automated reasoning tools for problems
that are [*NP-complete*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NP-completeness),
or lie at the first or second level
of the [*Polynomial Hierarchy*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_hierarchy)&mdash;which include
[*SAT solving algorithms*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conflict-driven_clause_learning),
[*constraint programming methods*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constraint_programming),
[*answer set programming methods*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Answer_set_programming),
and many more.

I am interested in showing how problems from other domains
(e.g., from various areas of symbolic AI) can be encoded into languages for which
efficient automated reasoning tools have been developed&mdash;and how
efficiently one can carry out such encodings.
This general approach can be used to provide a promising solving approach for many problems
that are computationally intractable in general.

The approach of efficiently encoding problems into another problem that can be solved with
sophisticated automated reasoning tools
motivated me to develop new parameterized complexity classes&mdash;and
write about them in my PhD thesis [De Haan, 2016].


## Logics

Various types of [*logic*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic)
play an important role in all of the topics mentioned above&mdash;moreover,
logic plays a key role in
[*theoretical computer science*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_computer_science).
As such, logic is central to my research interests.

In addition, I like to study the computational aspects&mdash;including
the computational complexity of satisfiability, model checking, and other reasoning problems,
as well as tradeoffs between compactness/expressivity and efficiency&mdash;of
various flavors of logics, such as:
- [*epistemic logics*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistemic_modal_logic)
(including [*dynamic epistemic logic*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamic_epistemic_logic)),
- [*description logics*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Description_logic),
- [*temporal logics*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temporal_logic),
- [*non-monotonic logics*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-monotonic_logic), and
- many more.


## Symbolic AI

(Most of) the above topics relate to
[symbolic (or good old-fashioned) artificial intelligence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbolic_artificial_intelligence).
I'm also interested more generally in symbolic AI.
For example, I'm interested in (computational aspects of) the following topics:
- [*planning*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automated_planning_and_scheduling),
- [*abductive reasoning*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abductive_reasoning),
- [*argumentation systems*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argumentation_framework), and
- many more.
